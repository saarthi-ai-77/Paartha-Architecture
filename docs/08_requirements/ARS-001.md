**Status: Active — Requirements and Functional Decomposition Both Drafted**

# ARS-001: Architecture Requirement Specification

**Purpose of this document:** answer "what must any successful ACA architecture be capable of doing," without assuming how — and then determine whether that answer itself reduces to a smaller set of fundamental computational functions. This is **not** an architecture document. It contains no modules, no diagrams, no interfaces, no implementation decisions.

**Method:** every mechanism, idea, and concept raised across prior research (developmental/mentor-education discussion, EXP-001 through EXP-004, and the architectural-properties discussion that preceded this document) was inventoried, then actively tested for reduction — merged wherever two named ideas turned out to be the same underlying capability, a phase of the same process, or a consequence/measurement of another capability rather than a mechanism in its own right (Section 1 → 15 requirements). A second, deeper reduction pass (Section 5) then asked whether those 15 requirements are themselves manifestations of a smaller set of implementation-independent functions — deliberately excluding the KFC's earlier, unsuccessful approach of chasing a single philosophical primitive (`council/knowledge-foundation/`), and instead requiring every candidate function to carry an explicit, engineering-usable input/output signature.

**Headline results:** approximately 30 distinct mechanisms/concepts reduce to **15 requirements across 5 domains** (Section 1–3); those 15 requirements reduce further to **4 fundamental functions — EVALUATE, SELECT, UPDATE, COMPOSE** (Section 5). One requirement (SR-01) — and, at the deeper level, one function (EVALUATE) — turned out to be a dependency of nearly everything else. Both reduction passes independently converge on the same conclusion. Architecture design may begin now, per Section 5.9.

---

## 1. Requirement Reduction — Showing the Work

This section exists because requirement reduction was named as one of the most important responsibilities of this document. Nothing below is asserted without the reasoning that produced it.

### 1.1 The Self-Regulation cluster: uncertainty, confidence, asking questions, self-testing, curiosity, detecting knowledge gaps, reflection, error correction, verification

These are not seven capabilities. They decompose into exactly two:

- A **calibrated, causally-usable self-evaluation signal** — an estimate of "is this correct / how confident am I," over any input, output, or internal state, that actually drives behavior rather than being an incidental byproduct read off after the fact. "Uncertainty" and "confidence" are the same signal described in negative and positive terms. "Detecting knowledge gaps" is this same signal aggregated over a domain rather than applied to a single input. "Verification" is this same signal applied specifically to a reasoning output.
- A **policy that consumes that signal and acts on it.** "Asking clarifying questions" fires this policy on a fresh input under high uncertainty. "Curiosity" (per the intrinsic-motivation/developmental-robotics literature already reviewed for the mentor-paradigm evaluation) is this same policy used as an *exploration* objective — seek inputs that maximize expected reduction in uncertainty. "Self-testing" is this same policy applied by actively generating or selecting novel probe inputs rather than waiting for one to arrive. "Reflection" is this same policy applied retrospectively to the system's own past output/trace instead of a fresh input. "Error correction" is the downstream action this policy triggers once a problem is flagged, which routes into the Memory domain's write pipeline (Section 1.2).

Seven candidate items reduce to two requirements (SR-01, SR-02).

### 1.2 The Memory cluster: episodic memory, fast writes, correction learning, replay, consolidation, salience, retrieval, persistent memory, semantic memory

Per the hint in the original directive, these are phases of one system, not separate systems — with one genuine exception.

- "Persistent memory" is not its own requirement — it is simply the property that follows from having any durable storage substrate at all; it is subsumed rather than restated.
- "Fast writes" and "correction learning" are the same thing: a write to episodic storage, gated by the self-evaluation signal (SR-01) detecting surprise/error — EXP-001 validated exactly this mechanism.
- "Replay" and "consolidation" are the same transfer process described from two angles (the neuroscience mechanism vs. its functional purpose): moving content from fast, individuated storage into slow, generalized storage over time.
- "Salience" is not a separate memory system — it is the gating criterion the write and consolidation processes both need (EXP-001's surprise-threshold-for-writing and mastery-threshold-for-eviction are literally two salience criteria for one pipeline).
- "Retrieval" is the read side of the same system — structurally a content-addressing operation.

What does **not** reduce: **episodic storage** (fast, individuated, low-sample-count writes; must tolerate high interference risk to write quickly) and **semantic storage** (slow, generalizing, compressed) remain genuinely separate, because continual-learning literature has repeatedly shown these two properties trade off against each other in a single system (fast, low-interference writing and generalization are in tension — this is *why* Complementary Learning Systems theory posits two systems rather than one compromise system). Nine candidate items reduce to four requirements (ME-01 through ME-04).

### 1.3 Abstraction, transfer, generalization

These reduce to **zero new requirements.** They are not mechanisms — they are the success criteria that the Reasoning & Computation domain's requirements (Section 1.4) must satisfy, measured via the Self-Regulation domain's self-testing application (SR-02). EXP-002 already demonstrated this concretely: "abstraction" there was literally "constrain the operator to the true symmetry family, discarding excess degrees of freedom," and the *outcome* of that abstraction was generalization to held-out combinations. Naming abstraction/transfer/generalization as separate requirements would double-count an evaluation criterion as if it were a mechanism.

### 1.4 The Reasoning + Computation clusters: compositional reasoning, multi-step reasoning, heterogeneous computation, symbolic-like reasoning, planning, verification, dynamic scheduling, adaptive compute, sparse activation, efficient routing, scalable inference

- "Symbolic-like reasoning where appropriate" and "compositional reasoning" are not separate requirements — they are descriptions of what happens when a domain is given a correctly structure-matched computation (RC-01, EXP-002-validated), rather than a generic one.
- "Dynamic scheduling," "adaptive compute," "sparse activation," and "efficient routing" are one requirement viewed from four angles: a validation-driven (not loss-driven, per EXP-003) selector across a heterogeneous computation library, additionally allocating variable compute per input. "Scalable inference" is a cross-cutting engineering constraint on this requirement, not a separate capability.
- "Multi-step reasoning" reduces almost entirely to two other requirements working together: composability (RC-01) plus a state-carrying mechanism across steps — which, on inspection, is simply the episodic storage substrate (ME-01) used for short-horizon state rather than long-horizon facts. It is kept as a distinct line (RC-03) only to make this cross-reference explicit, not because it needs new machinery.
- "Verification" reduces to the self-evaluation signal (SR-01) applied to a reasoning output — not a separate mechanism.
- "Planning" is the one item in this cluster that does **not** fully reduce. It requires composability, state-carrying, and the self-evaluation signal, but it also needs something none of those provide on their own: a search process over a combinatorial space of candidate composed sequences, scored by that self-evaluation signal. This survives as its own requirement (RC-04), with heavy, explicit dependencies on the others.

Eleven candidate items reduce to four requirements (RC-01 through RC-04, one of which — RC-03 — is flagged as ~90% reducible to ME-01).

### 1.5 The Learning cluster: continual learning, one-shot correction learning, mentor-guided education, transfer learning, abstraction, curriculum progression, lifelong learning

- "One-shot correction learning," "transfer learning," and "abstraction" are already accounted for above (Sections 1.2 and 1.3) — restating them here would double-count them.
- "Continual learning" and "lifelong learning" are the same property under two names: the ability to accumulate new competence over an unbounded period without destructively overwriting previously consolidated competence (the "stability-plasticity dilemma" from continual-learning literature).
- "Mentor-guided education" and "curriculum progression" are not, on inspection, architecture requirements at all in the form named — they are properties of a *training process*. The underlying architectural capability they actually require is narrower and more precise: the learning/update mechanism must be able to consume a **non-stationary, trajectory-dependent training signal** — one where the target depends on the system's own past outputs, not a fixed i.i.d. dataset. A curriculum or a mentor is one way to *produce* such a signal; the architecture's requirement is to be able to *consume* it, regardless of whether it's produced by a human curriculum, a mentor model, or something else.

Seven candidate items reduce to two requirements (LN-01, LN-02).

### 1.6 The Deployment cluster: graduation, continual adaptation, version evolution, deployment artifact, stability

- "Continual adaptation" post-deployment is not new — it is LN-01 and LN-02 continuing to hold after whatever event counts as deployment, not a separate capability that switches on at that point.
- "Stability" (behavioral consistency over time) is a named facet of LN-02 (the stability-plasticity property), not a separate requirement.
- "Graduation" reduces almost entirely to SR-02's self-testing application, packaged as a go/no-go signal — kept as its own line (DP-03) only because it was named and discussed repeatedly enough across this research to warrant an explicit, flagged cross-reference rather than silent disappearance.
- "Deployment artifact" is the one genuinely new requirement in this cluster: once competence lives across at least two storage substrates (episodic, semantic) plus routing/selection state plus a self-model, "what gets deployed" is no longer just "save the weights" — it requires defining what the deployable, coherent, portable unit actually is.
- "Version evolution" — traceable, potentially reversible checkpointing of the system's competence state over time — is related to but distinct from LN-02 (LN-02 is about *not losing* competence; this is about being able to *audit and roll back* competence state, echoing this repository's own historical-integrity principle applied to a running system rather than a document).

Five candidate items reduce to three requirements (DP-01 through DP-03).

## 2. Requirements

Each requirement is tagged with a confidence level: **Validated by ACA experiments**, **Supported by external literature**, **Reasoned hypothesis**, or **Speculative**. Several requirements carry a *partial* tag — validated for one half of the claim, reasoned for the rest — stated explicitly rather than rounded up.

### Domain: Learning (LN)

---
**LN-01 — Non-Stationary, Trajectory-Dependent Learning Signal Support**

*Description:* The architecture's learning/update mechanism must be able to consume a training signal whose target depends on the system's own prior outputs, not only a fixed, i.i.d. dataset.

*Problem solved:* Standard supervised/next-token training assumes a stationary target distribution. A mentor's next correction — or any interactive teaching process — depends on what the system just did, which a stationary-objective architecture cannot natively represent as a first-class case rather than an approximation (e.g. RLHF bolted onto a stationary-pretrained base).

*Why the requirement exists:* Without this, "mentor-guided" or "curriculum-driven" training can only ever be applied as a post-hoc adjustment layer on top of an architecture fundamentally built for a different (stationary) problem.

*Scientific support:* Well-established in reinforcement learning and active learning theory (non-stationary MDPs, off-policy correction). Related to, but independently grounded from, the "target depends on the learner's own trajectory" framing raised in ACA's quarantined `council/main` material — that framing is not being relied upon as evidence here, since its provenance is unresolved; the underlying claim is standard RL theory regardless.

*ACA evidence:* None directly. EXP-001 through EXP-004 all used stationary training objectives.

*External evidence:* Extensive — the entire non-stationary RL and active learning literature.

*Confidence level:* **Supported by external literature.**

*Open questions:* Does this require a different training objective only, or a different architectural mechanism (e.g. a distinct "policy" component vs. a "world model" component)? Untested for ACA's specific components.

*Dependencies:* Interacts with ME-03 (the write/consolidation pipeline must be drivable by a non-stationary signal, not just a stationary loss) and, more speculatively, RC-02 (whether the *correct* computation family for a domain could itself change over time — currently assumed not to, but not verified).

*Potential conflicts:* A learning mechanism built to consume a non-stationary, externally-supplied signal is also a mechanism that could in principle be steered adversarially by a malicious or degraded mentor — a real, currently unaddressed safety consideration, relevant to why the (still-inactive) Normative Foundation Council exists.

*Implementation independence:* Could be satisfied by an RL-style policy-gradient mechanism, an online meta-learning loop, or a bespoke trajectory-conditioned loss — the requirement names none of these.

---
**LN-02 — Stability-Plasticity Balance (Continual Learning Without Catastrophic Forgetting)**

*Description:* The architecture must be able to accumulate new competence over an unbounded period without destructively overwriting previously consolidated competence.

*Problem solved:* Catastrophic forgetting — a well-documented failure mode where training on new data overwrites prior learning in standard neural networks.

*Why the requirement exists:* A developmental process spanning an extended period is meaningless if later lessons erase earlier ones.

*Scientific support:* Decades-established: McCloskey & Cohen (1989), French (1999), and the broader continual-learning literature.

*ACA evidence:* None directly — EXP-001's memory allocator manages new facts under fixed capacity but was never tested for whether adding facts over time degrades previously-learned ones in the *semantic* (parametric) component.

*External evidence:* Extensive continual-learning literature, including the CLS-theory-inspired "fast and slow" continual learning models (e.g. DualNet, arXiv 2110.00175) already surfaced in the prior architectural-properties discussion.

*Confidence level:* **Supported by external literature**, with ACA-specific applicability untested.

*Open questions:* Whether ME-01/ME-02's two-substrate split is sufficient to satisfy this on its own, or whether an additional explicit anti-forgetting mechanism (e.g. elastic weight consolidation-style regularization) is needed on top.

*Dependencies:* Heavily overlaps with ME-03 — this may be best understood as a *constraint that ME-03 must satisfy* rather than a fully independent mechanism, flagged honestly rather than presented as clean separation.

*Potential conflicts:* Directly in tension with ME-01's requirement for fast, low-friction writes — writing quickly and never forgetting anything are not simultaneously free; some forgetting/consolidation policy is unavoidable, and getting it wrong in either direction (too little forgetting = unbounded growth; too much = the catastrophic-forgetting failure this requirement exists to prevent) is a real risk.

*Implementation independence:* Could be satisfied by rehearsal/replay, regularization-based approaches (EWC, synaptic intelligence), architectural isolation (progressive networks), or something not yet named in the literature.

### Domain: Memory (ME)

---
**ME-01 — Episodic Storage Substrate**

*Description:* A storage mechanism capable of recording specific, individuated experiences or facts from a small number of exposures (ideally one), without requiring many repetitions to become reliably retrievable.

*Problem solved:* Standard gradient-descent-only parametric learning requires many repeated exposures to reliably encode a rare or one-off fact — a fundamental sample-inefficiency for long-tail knowledge.

*Why the requirement exists:* Corrections, one-off facts, and short-horizon working state all need to be captured without waiting for many repetitions.

*Scientific support:* Complementary Learning Systems theory (McClelland, McNaughton & O'Reilly, 1995; updated Kumaran, Hassabis & McClelland, 2016) — hippocampal fast, pattern-separated encoding.

*ACA evidence:* **Directly validated by EXP-001** — a fixed-capacity key-value store, written on high prediction-error, achieved ~2x the tail-fact recall of naive caching at equal capacity.

*External evidence:* CLS theory; kNN-LM, RETRO (retrieval-augmented generation showing smaller parametric models + retrieval matching larger pure-parametric ones).

*Confidence level:* **Validated by ACA experiments**, at small toy-task scale with near-orthogonal (effectively exact-match) keys — retrieval robustness under realistic, confusable keys remains untested (flagged in EXP-001's own limitations).

*Open questions:* Behavior under non-orthogonal/confusable keys; capacity scaling behavior; whether the same substrate serves both "correction learning" and "short-horizon working state during multi-step reasoning" (RC-03) equally well, or whether these are actually two different usage patterns needing different tuning of the same underlying mechanism.

*Dependencies:* Feeds ME-03 (write side) and ME-04 (read side); required by RC-03.

*Potential conflicts:* See LN-02 (stability-plasticity tension with ME-02).

*Implementation independence:* Could be a literal key-value dictionary (as in EXP-001's toy implementation), a differentiable addressable memory (Neural Turing Machine/DNC-style), or a vector database with approximate nearest-neighbor retrieval.

---
**ME-02 — Semantic Storage Substrate**

*Description:* A storage mechanism (in practice, a set of slowly-updating trainable parameters) that accumulates generalized, compressed regularities across many exposures.

*Problem solved:* Individuated, uncompressed episodic storage does not generalize; without a slow-consolidating counterpart, the system cannot form reusable, compact competence.

*Why the requirement exists:* This is the counterpart CLS theory (and continual-learning practice generally) requires alongside ME-01 — not a novel claim on its own, but necessary to state explicitly since the point is that it must coexist with, not replace, ME-01.

*Scientific support:* This is standard practice in essentially all of deep learning (parametric weights trained by gradient descent) — trivially well-supported, included here for completeness of the pairing with ME-01.

*ACA evidence:* Every experiment (EXP-001 through EXP-004) used a parametric backbone of this kind as a baseline/foundation.

*External evidence:* The entirety of standard deep learning practice; CLS theory's "neocortex" half specifically.

*Confidence level:* **Supported by external literature** (essentially uncontroversial in isolation; the requirement is really about its *coexistence* with ME-01, covered under ME-03/LN-02).

*Open questions:* None specific to this component in isolation; open questions live in how it interacts with ME-01/ME-03.

*Dependencies:* Feeds ME-03 (as the consolidation target) and RC-01 (constrained computation families still need some substrate to store their learned parameters — this is likely it, for the "family" parameters themselves, distinct from the episodic facts they operate over).

*Potential conflicts:* See LN-02.

*Implementation independence:* Could be conventional dense parameters, a mixture-of-experts parameter set, or any other slowly-trained representation.

---
**ME-03 — Salience-Gated Write/Consolidation Pipeline**

*Description:* A process that (a) writes to episodic storage (ME-01) when the semantic substrate (ME-02) currently fails on an input, gated by a salience/surprise criterion, and (b) transfers/consolidates episodic content into the semantic substrate over time, gated by a mastery/redundancy criterion, freeing episodic capacity.

*Problem solved:* Neither "always write everything" nor "never consolidate" is viable — capacity is finite and naive policies waste it (demonstrated concretely, not assumed).

*Why the requirement exists:* This is the connective tissue between ME-01 and ME-02 — without it, they are two unconnected stores rather than one coherent memory system.

*Scientific support:* CLS theory's consolidation/replay mechanism; directly informed by ACA's own result.

*ACA evidence:* **Validated by ACA experiments for the write-gating half** — EXP-001's surprise-gated write policy achieved ~2x the tail-fact recall of naive caching at equal capacity, specifically by avoiding writes/retention for facts the semantic substrate already handles (naive caching wasted 73% of capacity there; the gated policy wasted 2%). **Not validated for the full bidirectional consolidation half** — EXP-001 only evicts (frees capacity once the backbone independently learns a fact); it does not actively transfer episodic content *into* the semantic substrate's weights. That directional gap is a reasoned extension, not yet tested.

*External evidence:* CLS theory; DualNet and related "fast and slow" continual learning architectures.

*Confidence level:* **Validated by ACA experiments (partial — write/eviction gating only); consolidation-transfer half is a Reasoned hypothesis.**

*Open questions:* How to make the semantic-transfer direction explicit and active (rather than passive/incidental, as in EXP-001) is flagged as a concrete, buildable next experiment.

*Dependencies:* Requires ME-01, ME-02, and SR-01 (the salience/surprise/mastery criteria *are* an application of the self-evaluation signal — this is the first of several places SR-01 turns out to be load-bearing, see Section 3).

*Potential conflicts:* Directly implements the LN-02 stability-plasticity tension; also interacts with LN-01 if the gating criteria themselves need to adapt to a non-stationary signal (untested).

*Implementation independence:* Could use a fixed threshold (as in EXP-001's toy implementation), a learned gating network, or a Bayesian/probabilistic salience estimate.

---
**ME-04 — Content-Addressable Retrieval**

*Description:* Given a query, find and return the relevant stored content from episodic and/or semantic storage.

*Problem solved:* Storage without retrieval is useless; this is the read side of the memory system.

*Why the requirement exists:* Distinct from write/consolidation (ME-03) — a system could in principle write well and retrieve poorly (e.g. via inadequate similarity metrics), so this is worth stating as its own requirement even though it's tightly coupled to ME-01.

*Scientific support:* Content-addressable memory is a long-established concept; attention mechanisms are a well-known instance of it.

*ACA evidence:* EXP-001 used exact-match retrieval (a simplification enabled by near-orthogonal keys) — the general, non-exact-match case is untested by ACA.

*External evidence:* kNN-LM, RETRO, and the broader retrieval-augmented generation literature; attention mechanisms generally.

*Confidence level:* **Validated by ACA experiments (narrow, exact-match case); Supported by external literature for the general case.**

*Open questions:* Retrieval robustness under confusable/non-orthogonal keys — explicitly flagged as untested in EXP-001's own limitations section.

*Dependencies:* Requires ME-01 (and optionally ME-02) to have content to retrieve from.

*Potential conflicts:* None distinctly new beyond general retrieval-accuracy-vs-speed tradeoffs.

*Implementation independence:* Could be exact dictionary lookup, approximate nearest-neighbor search, or a learned attention-based retrieval mechanism.

### Domain: Reasoning & Computation (RC) — merged domain, see Section 3

---
**RC-01 — Heterogeneous, Structure-Matched Computation Library**

*Description:* The architecture must support multiple, structurally different computation types (not one uniform mechanism applied to everything), each constrained to match the true structure of the domain it handles.

*Problem solved:* A single, generic, high-parameter computation does not reliably generalize compositionally — it has enough free parameters to fit training data via many solutions, only some of which generalize.

*Why the requirement exists:* Demonstrated directly, not assumed.

*Scientific support:* Directly informed by ACA's own falsification-then-confirmation result; independently supported by the Fodor & Pylyshyn systematicity critique and the Geometric Deep Learning research program (symmetry-derived architectures).

*ACA evidence:* **Validated by ACA experiments** — EXP-002: a generic factorization failed to generalize compositionally across three separate attempts (learned embedding, fixed features, weight-decay sweep, all exactly 0% held-out accuracy); constraining the operator to its true symmetry family (a 2-parameter rotation/reflection) gave exact, parameter-correct generalization.

*External evidence:* Fodor & Pylyshyn (1988); Geometric Deep Learning (Bronstein, Bruna, Cohen, Veličković); SCAN-benchmark compositional-generalization-failure literature.

*Confidence level:* **Validated by ACA experiments** (on a small synthetic task; not yet tested at scale or on a richer family library).

*Open questions:* How is the correct family for an unfamiliar domain discovered or constructed, rather than selected from a pre-supplied library? This is the central open problem flagged by EXP-002/003 and tracked as EXP-005 in the roadmap.

*Dependencies:* None as a foundational matter, though its practical usefulness depends on RC-02 (a library with no working selection mechanism is not usable).

*Potential conflicts:* A larger, richer library increases the difficulty of RC-02's selection problem — more candidate families to choose among, more room for the selection mechanism to fail the way EXP-003's naive/parsimony conditions did.

*Implementation independence:* Could be implemented as a fixed hand-designed set of constrained modules, a learned but structurally-typed module library, or a program-synthesis-style generative family space.

---
**RC-02 — Validation-Driven Selection with Variable Compute Allocation**

*Description:* A mechanism that selects which computation (from RC-01's library) handles a given input, and how much computation to apply, driven by measured held-out generalization performance rather than training loss or a fixed complexity penalty.

*Problem solved:* Naive, loss-driven selection reliably collapses toward the most expressive (least generalizing) option; a fixed-strength complexity penalty does not reliably fix this either.

*Why the requirement exists:* Demonstrated directly, not assumed.

*Scientific support:* Directly informed by ACA's own result.

*ACA evidence:* **Validated by ACA experiments for the "which family" half** — EXP-003: naive joint training and parsimony-regularized training both measured at exactly 0% held-out accuracy across all tested settings; a held-out-validation-driven selector matched oracle (hand-picked) accuracy exactly. **Not validated for the "how much compute" half** — EXP-003 only tested discrete selection among families of fixed cost, not variable compute allocation per input.

*External evidence:* General model-selection/regularization literature showing small-weight and generalizing solutions do not reliably coincide outside specific provable cases.

*Confidence level:* **Validated by ACA experiments (partial — family selection only); variable compute allocation is a Reasoned hypothesis.**

*Open questions:* Whether the same validation-driven principle holds when the decision is made per-input at inference time (as a real scheduler would need) rather than per-operator during training (as EXP-003 tested); whether it holds when routing among more than two candidate options.

*Dependencies:* Requires RC-01 (a library to select from) and SR-01 (the "measured held-out performance" signal is itself an application of the self-evaluation capability — the second place SR-01 turns out to be load-bearing).

*Potential conflicts:* Directly in tension with scalable inference / latency constraints (DP-adjacent) — allocating more compute to harder inputs threatens throughput guarantees a real deployed system would need.

*Implementation independence:* Could be a learned gating network, an explicit validation-split evaluation procedure run at training time, or a runtime confidence-based early-exit mechanism.

---
**RC-03 — Sequential State-Carrying for Multi-Step Composition**

*Description:* A mechanism for carrying intermediate results across a chain of composed computation steps.

*Problem solved:* Multi-step reasoning needs somewhere to hold partial results between steps.

*Why the requirement exists:* Named as its own line only to make an important cross-reference explicit — on inspection, this reduces almost entirely to ME-01 (episodic storage) used for short-horizon rather than long-horizon content. It is not new machinery.

*Scientific support:* Working-memory literature in cognitive science; standard sequence-model hidden-state mechanisms in ML.

*ACA evidence:* None directly — no ACA experiment has tested multi-step composition specifically.

*External evidence:* Extensive (recurrent/working-memory literature generally).

*Confidence level:* **Reasoned hypothesis** — the reduction to ME-01 itself is reasoned, not tested; it may turn out that short-horizon working state and long-horizon fact storage need different tuning of the same substrate, which would partially undo this reduction.

*Open questions:* Whether ME-01's validated design (capacity-constrained, competence-gated) is actually well-suited to short-horizon working state, which has different demands (fast overwrite, no need for long-term retention) than long-tail fact storage.

*Dependencies:* ME-01; requires SR-01 for a termination criterion (knowing when a chain of steps is "done").

*Potential conflicts:* None beyond those already listed under ME-01.

*Implementation independence:* Could be a scratchpad/stack structure, recurrent hidden state, or literally the same episodic store as ME-01 with a different write policy.

---
**RC-04 — Search Over Composed Sequences (Planning)**

*Description:* A mechanism for generating and evaluating multiple candidate composed action/reasoning sequences, scored by the self-evaluation signal, to select one that achieves a goal.

*Problem solved:* Composability and state-carrying alone do not provide a way to *choose among* multiple candidate multi-step sequences — that requires an explicit search/optimization process.

*Why the requirement exists:* This is the one item in the Reasoning cluster that does not fully reduce to the others — it depends on them but adds genuinely new machinery (a search process over a combinatorial space).

*Scientific support:* Classical planning and search literature (e.g. Monte Carlo Tree Search, A*) is extensive and well-established, though not yet connected to ACA's own validated mechanisms.

*ACA evidence:* None.

*External evidence:* Extensive classical AI planning/search literature.

*Confidence level:* **Reasoned hypothesis**, informed by well-established external planning/search literature but with no ACA-specific test.

*Open questions:* Whether search needs to be a distinct architectural mechanism or could emerge from RC-01/RC-02/RC-03 applied repeatedly with a self-evaluation-driven stopping rule.

*Dependencies:* RC-01, RC-03, SR-01 (scoring candidate trajectories).

*Potential conflicts:* Search is typically expensive — direct tension with RC-02's compute-allocation and any latency/throughput constraint.

*Implementation independence:* Could be tree search, sampling-based rollout comparison, or a learned value function guiding a single forward pass.

### Domain: Self-Regulation (SR)

---
**SR-01 — Calibrated, Causally-Active Self-Evaluation Signal**

*Description:* An estimate of "is this correct / how confident am I," computable over any input, output, or internal state, whose value actually influences downstream behavior rather than being an incidental, unused byproduct.

*Problem solved:* Without this, a system cannot know when to write to memory, when to consolidate, which computation family actually generalizes, when to ask for help, or when a reasoning chain should stop — all of which require some notion of "is this right," not just "is this the highest-probability output."

*Why the requirement exists:* See Section 3 — this turned out to be a dependency of ME-03, RC-02, RC-03, and RC-04, not merely a Self-Regulation-domain concern.

*Scientific support:* Calibration literature in ML; metacognition literature in cognitive science.

*ACA evidence:* **A narrow instance is validated by EXP-001** — the surprise-threshold mechanism that gates memory writes is exactly this signal, in miniature. However, EXP-001's threshold was a fixed hyperparameter applied to one specific loss, not a general-purpose, calibrated signal usable across arbitrary inputs and internal states — that generalization is reasoned, not validated.

*External evidence:* Extensive calibration and uncertainty-estimation literature; well-documented finding that raw softmax probabilities are poorly calibrated and that RLHF-style post-training tends to further miscalibrate them.

*Confidence level:* **Validated by ACA experiments (narrow instance only); generalization to a broad, general-purpose signal is a Reasoned hypothesis.**

*Open questions:* Whether one signal can serve all the downstream consumers (memory gating, family selection, planning termination, help-seeking) or whether these actually need distinct, differently-calibrated signals — this document currently assumes one signal suffices, but this assumption itself has not been tested and is worth stating as a hypothesis, not a given.

*Dependencies:* None as a foundational matter — this is the most upstream, most foundational requirement in the entire specification (see Section 3).

*Potential conflicts:* A signal that is too conservative (over-flags uncertainty) makes the system unusable (SR-02, below) via excessive question-asking or excessive memory writes; too liberal, and it fails at the job it exists to do. Calibration quality is not free.

*Implementation independence:* Could be implemented via ensembling, a learned auxiliary calibration head, Bayesian approximation, or a fixed-threshold heuristic (as in EXP-001's minimal version) — the requirement does not specify which.

---
**SR-02 — Confidence-Conditioned Action Policy**

*Description:* A policy that consumes SR-01's signal and selects an action: proceed, ask a clarifying question, actively probe for novel test cases (self-testing), retrospectively examine a past trace (reflection), or trigger a correction (routing into ME-03).

*Problem solved:* A calibrated signal with nothing acting on it is inert — this is the behavioral half of self-regulation.

*Why the requirement exists:* Distinguishes "knowing you don't know" from "doing something appropriate about it," which are logically separable capabilities (a system could have perfect calibration and still have no mechanism to act on it).

*Scientific support:* Intrinsic-motivation/curiosity-driven learning literature (Oudeyer et al., already reviewed for the mentor-paradigm evaluation); active learning literature.

*ACA evidence:* None directly — EXP-001's write-gating is arguably a minimal instance (act by writing, conditioned on the surprise signal), but no ACA experiment has tested question-asking, active self-testing, or reflection specifically.

*External evidence:* Developmental robotics' intrinsic-motivation literature; active learning literature.

*Confidence level:* **Reasoned hypothesis, supported by external literature.**

*Open questions:* Whether one policy genuinely covers all five named behaviors (question-asking, curiosity-exploration, self-testing, reflection, error-correction-triggering) or whether some of these need distinct policies despite consuming the same underlying signal.

*Dependencies:* SR-01.

*Potential conflicts:* Excessive question-asking or excessive self-generated-test-probing could make a deployed system impractically slow or intrusive — a real usability/autonomy tension, not just a technical one.

*Implementation independence:* Could be a learned RL policy over actions, a rule-based threshold policy, or an emergent property of a broader planning mechanism (RC-04).

### Domain: Deployment (DP)

---
**DP-01 — Multi-Substrate Deployment Artifact**

*Description:* A definition of what the deployable, portable, executable unit actually consists of, given that competence lives across at least the episodic substrate (ME-01), the semantic substrate (ME-02), routing/selection state (RC-02), and a self-evaluation signal (SR-01) — not just a single set of frozen weights.

*Problem solved:* The standard "freeze the weights, deploy the checkpoint" abstraction does not obviously extend to a system with multiple heterogeneous, differently-updating components.

*Why the requirement exists:* Raised explicitly in the mentor-education proposal evaluation as an open question ("what exactly graduates") and confirmed, on reflection, to be a genuine architectural gap rather than a solved problem with an obvious answer.

*Scientific support:* Related to, but not resolved by, the fast-weights/test-time-training literature (e.g. arXiv 2310.13807), which addresses adaptable weights but not the broader multi-substrate packaging question.

*ACA evidence:* None.

*External evidence:* Fast weights / test-time-training / weight-space meta-learning literature addresses adjacent pieces but not this specific packaging question directly.

*Confidence level:* **Speculative.**

*Open questions:* Essentially all of them — this is the least-resolved requirement in the specification.

*Dependencies:* ME-01, ME-02, RC-02, SR-01 — this requirement's entire content is "package all of the above coherently," so it depends on all of them existing and being reasonably stable first.

*Potential conflicts:* Directly in tension with DP-02 (a continually-updating multi-substrate system is a moving target, making clean versioned checkpoints harder to define).

*Implementation independence:* Genuinely unresolved — this is exactly the kind of question this document is supposed to leave open rather than answer prematurely.

---
**DP-02 — Versioned, Traceable Competence Checkpoints**

*Description:* The ability to checkpoint the system's full competence state at points in time, in a way that is auditable and potentially reversible.

*Problem solved:* A continually-updating system (per LN-01/LN-02) needs some way to know what it knew at a given point, and to roll back if an update degrades behavior.

*Why the requirement exists:* Standard ML practice already checkpoints weights; this extends that practice to a multi-substrate, continually-updating system where "the weights" is no longer the whole story.

*Scientific support:* Standard ML checkpointing/versioning practice, extended (not invented) here.

*ACA evidence:* None.

*External evidence:* Standard ML engineering practice for checkpointing; no specific literature found addressing the multi-substrate extension directly.

*Confidence level:* **Reasoned hypothesis.**

*Open questions:* What granularity of versioning is meaningful for a system with fast (episodic) and slow (semantic) components updating at very different rates.

*Dependencies:* LN-02, DP-01.

*Potential conflicts:* See DP-01.

*Implementation independence:* Could be full-state snapshots, incremental diffs, or a hybrid — unresolved by design.

---
**DP-03 — Deployment-Readiness / Graduation Signal**

*Description:* A machine-checkable signal, derived from SR-02's self-testing application, indicating that the system's demonstrated generalization on novel compositions meets some threshold, usable as an input to a deployment go/no-go decision.

*Problem solved:* "Graduation" as discussed in the mentor-education evaluation is otherwise an informal, unfalsifiable notion.

*Why the requirement exists:* Kept as an explicit, separate line — despite reducing almost entirely to SR-02 — because the concept was named and discussed repeatedly enough across this research program to warrant a direct, flagged cross-reference rather than silently disappearing into another requirement's description.

*Scientific support:* None specific — this is the most purely reasoned, least externally-grounded requirement in the specification.

*ACA evidence:* None. EXP-002/003's held-out evaluation methodology is the closest precedent, but it was run by researchers offline, not by the system itself as an internal signal.

*External evidence:* None found directly addressing this framing.

*Confidence level:* **Speculative.**

*Open questions:* What threshold, over what distribution of self-generated test cases, would constitute sufficient evidence — this is a decision-threshold problem structurally similar to the one the (now-superseded) Knowledge Foundation Council's Sprint 3A wrestled with for validating primitives, worth noting as a recurring pattern in this research program rather than a coincidence.

*Dependencies:* SR-01, SR-02, DP-01.

*Potential conflicts:* A miscalibrated readiness signal (SR-01 itself being imperfect) directly undermines any decision built on top of it — this requirement inherits all of SR-01's open questions and confidence caveats.

*Implementation independence:* Could be a fixed accuracy threshold on a held-out probe set, a statistical confidence bound, or a human-in-the-loop sign-off informed by the signal rather than replaced by it.

## 3. Dependency Graph and Derived Domain Structure

### 3.1 The most important finding: SR-01 is not a domain-local concern

The dependency analysis above surfaces one result more clearly than any individual requirement: **SR-01 (the self-evaluation signal) is a direct dependency of ME-03, RC-02, RC-03, RC-04, and DP-03** — that is, of every domain except Learning. It is not "the Self-Regulation domain's internal business" — it is closer to a foundational, cross-cutting capability that the rest of the specification is built on top of. If this specification were re-derived from scratch starting purely from dependency structure rather than from the six domains originally proposed, SR-01 would likely sit at the root of the graph, with Memory, Reasoning & Computation, and Deployment all drawing from it. This is stated as the single most load-bearing finding of this document, not a minor observation.

### 3.2 Why Reasoning and Computation were merged

The directive's proposed structure separated "Reasoning" and "Computation" into different domains. The reduction work in Section 1.4 found this separation does not survive contact with the actual dependency structure: "heterogeneous computation" (originally listed under Reasoning) and "dynamic scheduling / adaptive compute / sparse activation / efficient routing" (originally listed under Computation) turned out to be the same requirement (RC-01/RC-02) viewed from two different angles — what to compute vs. how much/which. Keeping them as separate domains would have meant either duplicating the requirement or arbitrarily assigning it to one domain and cross-referencing the other. Merging them is the structure the requirements themselves produced, not a stylistic preference.

### 3.3 Foundational vs. derived requirements

- **Foundational (no upstream dependencies):** SR-01, ME-01, ME-02, RC-01, LN-01 (though LN-01 is more orthogonal/interacting than depended-upon by the others).
- **First-order derived (depend only on foundational requirements):** ME-03 (← ME-01, ME-02, SR-01), ME-04 (← ME-01), RC-02 (← RC-01, SR-01), SR-02 (← SR-01), LN-02 (heavily overlapping with ME-03).
- **Second-order derived:** RC-03 (← ME-01, SR-01), RC-04 (← RC-01, RC-03, SR-01), DP-03 (← SR-01, SR-02).
- **Third-order / integrative:** DP-01 (← ME-01, ME-02, RC-02, SR-01 — packages nearly everything), DP-02 (← LN-02, DP-01).

### 3.4 Natural subsystem clusters (derived, not assumed)

1. **Evaluation Core:** SR-01 alone — foundational enough to be its own cluster rather than folded into "Self-Regulation" as originally framed.
2. **Memory & Consolidation:** ME-01, ME-02, ME-03, ME-04 — tightly coupled, largely self-contained aside from their dependency on the Evaluation Core.
3. **Structured Computation:** RC-01, RC-02, RC-03, RC-04 — dependent on the Evaluation Core and on Memory (RC-03 specifically).
4. **Learning Dynamics:** LN-01, LN-02 — less a clean subsystem than a set of cross-cutting constraints on Memory & Consolidation and on the training process generally; noted honestly as not forming as clean a cluster as the others.
5. **Deployment/Packaging:** DP-01, DP-02, DP-03 — sits on top of everything else; cannot be specified further until the other clusters stabilize.

## 5. Functional Decomposition: From Requirements to Fundamental Functions

Section 1 reduced mechanisms to requirements. This section asks the same question one level deeper: are the 15 requirements themselves manifestations of a smaller set of *fundamental computational functions* — more abstract than a mechanism (not "attention," not "a key-value store"), less abstract than a philosophical primitive (not "Distinction," the concept the Knowledge Foundation Council spent three sprints failing to validate — see `council/knowledge-foundation/`), and directly useful for engineering, meaning each candidate function must have an explicit input/output signature that could be implemented, tested, and swapped independently of the others.

### 5.1 Criterion for admitting a function to this list

A candidate is admitted only if it has: (a) a clear, typed input/output signature; (b) at least two plausible, genuinely different implementations (if only one implementation is conceivable, the "function" is really just that mechanism wearing an abstract name); (c) demonstrated necessity — shown by tracing specific ARS-001 requirements that need it, not asserted.

### 5.2 The Four Functions

**EVALUATE** — `(Representation, Reference | Representation) → Score`
Produces a scalar (or low-dimensional) judgment of discrepancy, match, or quality between a representation and either another representation or an implicit/learned reference. Pairwise or reflexive; does not itself modify any persistent state.

**SELECT** — `(Set[(Candidate, Score)]) → Choice | Weighting`
Reduces a scored set of alternatives to a decision: a single choice, a top-k subset, or a soft weighting. Strictly consumes EVALUATE's output — it has no independent way to compare candidates.

**UPDATE** — `(State, NewInformation, Gate) → State'`
Modifies a persistent state as a function of new information, conditioned on a gate value. This is the only one of the four functions with a *required* persistent side effect — the others can be (and often are) implemented as pure, stateless transforms.

**COMPOSE** — `(Representation(s), Rule) → Representation`
Combines one or more representations according to a — possibly heavily constrained — rule, producing a new representation of a kind usable for further processing (unlike EVALUATE, whose output is a judgment, not a reusable representation).

### 5.3 Why not fewer than four — merges attempted and rejected

Each pairwise merge below was actually attempted, not assumed away:

- **SELECT into EVALUATE?** Rejected. EVALUATE produces one score per candidate; SELECT is a genuinely separate operation over a *set* of scores (argmax, top-k, softmax-weighted mixture, tournament search), each with real, different engineering tradeoffs and failure modes (e.g. approximate nearest-neighbor search at scale is a nontrivial problem independent of how any individual score was computed). Collapsing them would hide a real design axis — exactly the axis EXP-003 already showed matters (family-selection *policy*, given the same underlying evaluation, was the difference between 0% and 75% held-out accuracy).
- **UPDATE into COMPOSE?** Rejected. `UPDATE(old_state, new_info, gate)` can superficially look like a composition of two operands, but COMPOSE is stateless by design in this decomposition — it produces an ephemeral output — while UPDATE's defining property is a *persistent, order-dependent side effect on a substrate that outlives the current computation*. Every concern in the Memory domain (capacity, interference, forgetting, consolidation direction) is a consequence of statefulness that a stateless composition rule does not have to contend with.
- **EVALUATE into COMPOSE?** Rejected. COMPOSE is structure-*preserving* (a rotation of a point yields another point, usable as input to a further rotation); EVALUATE is structure-*collapsing* (it takes a rich representation and returns a low-dimensional judgment not intended for further structural composition). Conflating a metric/distance function with an equivariant transform is mathematically and practically a category error.

No further merge survived scrutiny. Four is the smallest set that both (a) generates all 15 requirements (Section 5.5) and (b) preserves distinctions the experiments have already shown to matter.

### 5.4 Why not more than four — the boundary case considered and excluded

**Encoding** (mapping raw external input into an internal representation usable by the other four functions) was considered as a candidate fifth function. It is necessary for any system to function at all, but it is *not* included here, for a specific reason: none of ARS-001's 15 requirements is actually about how a representation comes into existence in the first place — all 15 presuppose representations already exist and describe what must be done *with* them. No ACA experiment (EXP-001–004) tested or challenged standard embedding/tokenization practice; nothing in the evidence base suggests it needs ACA-specific innovation. Encoding is therefore named here explicitly as a **boundary assumption** this decomposition takes for granted, not silently omitted and not force-fit into the four functions as if it had been derived from the same evidence.

### 5.5 Mapping: every requirement expressed as a composition of the four functions

| Requirement | Functional composition |
|---|---|
| ME-04 (Retrieval) | EVALUATE (query vs. each stored key) → SELECT (top match) |
| SR-01 (Self-evaluation signal) | *Is* EVALUATE, applied reflexively — this requirement is the requirements-level name for the function itself, not a further composition of it |
| SR-02 (Action policy) | EVALUATE (score each candidate behavior) → SELECT (choose a behavior) |
| ME-03 (Write/consolidation pipeline) | EVALUATE (salience/mastery) → SELECT (write vs. don't; consolidate vs. don't) → UPDATE (episodic and/or semantic state) |
| RC-01 (Computation library) | *Is* COMPOSE, in multiplicity (each "family" is one constrained composition rule) |
| RC-02 (Validation-driven selection + compute allocation) | EVALUATE (held-out performance per family; per compute-depth) → SELECT (family; compute budget), twice over two different candidate sets |
| RC-03 (Sequential state-carrying) | UPDATE (state at each step) interleaved with COMPOSE (the step's transform) |
| RC-04 (Planning/search) | COMPOSE (generate candidate sequences) → EVALUATE (score each) → SELECT (best sequence) |
| LN-01 (Non-stationary learning signal) | EVALUATE (against a target that is itself a function of trajectory) feeding UPDATE |
| DP-03 (Graduation signal) | EVALUATE (aggregated across many self-generated probes) → SELECT (threshold-crossing, i.e. a degenerate two-option SELECT) |
| LN-02 (Stability-plasticity) | Not a fifth function — a required *property/constraint on UPDATE's gate design*, not a separate operation |
| ME-01, ME-02 (Episodic, semantic storage) | Not functions — the persistent *state* that UPDATE writes to and EVALUATE/SELECT read from; state is presupposed by any function claiming a "State" argument |
| DP-01, DP-02 (Deployment artifact, versioned checkpoints) | Not reducible to the four functions — these are packaging/serialization concerns about the accumulated state the functions produce, orthogonal to the computations themselves (see 5.6) |

13 of 15 requirements resolve cleanly into combinations of exactly four functions. The remaining two (LN-02, and the ME-01/ME-02 storage substrates) are not counter-examples — they are, respectively, a *constraint on* a function and the *state* functions operate over, neither of which the reduction claimed to explain away.

### 5.6 What remains genuinely non-functional

**DP-01 and DP-02** do not reduce to EVALUATE/SELECT/UPDATE/COMPOSE, and forcing them to would be dishonest. They are concerns about how accumulated state gets serialized, exported, and versioned — an engineering/packaging layer sitting on top of whatever the four functions have produced, not a computation the four functions perform. This is stated plainly rather than papered over with a false reduction.

### 5.7 Function dependency structure — and where it converges with Section 3

- **COMPOSE** has no dependency on the other three — it can exist as a pure, fixed transform with no evaluation, selection, or state at all (a system that only composes, never adapts, is a degenerate but coherent case).
- **EVALUATE** likewise has no dependency on the other three.
- **SELECT strictly requires EVALUATE** — there is no way to choose among candidates without scores to choose by.
- **UPDATE, in its *gated* form (the only form any ARS-001 requirement actually needs), requires EVALUATE** — an ungated, unconditional write would not need it, but nothing in this specification calls for unconditional writes.

This means **EVALUATE is the one function on which both other non-independent functions (SELECT, UPDATE) depend, while COMPOSE remains independent** — the same conclusion Section 3.1 reached about SR-01 being the most load-bearing *requirement*, now re-derived one level deeper as a property of the *function* SR-01 was always naming. The two passes converge on the same point from different directions, which is itself evidence the convergence is real rather than an artifact of how Section 3's dependency graph happened to be drawn.

### 5.8 Epistemic status of this decomposition

This is a logical/engineering reduction, not an empirical claim, and is tagged accordingly rather than forced into the four-tier evidence scale used for object-level requirements: **sufficiency is demonstrated** (Section 5.5 shows, not asserts, that the four functions generate 13 of 15 requirements, with the remaining two explicitly categorized as a constraint and a state substrate rather than left unexplained); **minimality is argued, not proven** (Section 5.3's merge attempts are reasoning, not a formal proof that no smaller sufficient set exists). Future work that finds a smaller sufficient set, or a case none of the four covers, should revise this section rather than be treated as contradicting settled results.

### 5.9 Implication for Architecture Design

Architecture design may now begin. Its shape is already partly determined by this section, without this section having designed it: a concrete architecture must supply one implementation each for EVALUATE, SELECT, UPDATE, and COMPOSE (or several typed variants of each, as RC-01's "heterogeneous library" already requires for COMPOSE specifically), and must define the state substrates (episodic, semantic, routing/self-model) those implementations read and write. Every future architectural decision should be traceable to (a) which of the four functions it implements, (b) which ARS-001 requirement(s) that implementation is meant to satisfy, and (c) whether the evidence behind that requirement was Validated, Supported, Reasoned, or Speculative. A decision that cannot be traced this way has not been justified by this specification or its functional decomposition.
