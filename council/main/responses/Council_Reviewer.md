**Reframing acknowledged. This is now a much harder and much more legitimate question — and also one with more directly relevant prior art than the web-design version had. Let me be precise about what already exists before proposing categories.**

**Closest existing attempt at exactly this question**

The single nearest piece of prior work isn't in web design or even mainstream ML — it's Bronstein, Bruna, Cohen, and Veličković's "Geometric Deep Learning" program. Working in the spirit of Felix Klein's Erlangen Program, they propose to derive different inductive biases and network architectures from first principles of symmetry and invariance, rather than treating architecture choice as a matter of taste. Their stated motivation is almost identical to yours: the field has become a zoo of architectures with few unifying principles, causing the same ideas to be reinvented under different names across application domains. They land on four to five structural classes — grids, groups (homogeneous spaces with global symmetry), graphs/sets, and manifolds/meshes (geodesics and gauges) — each with a derived representation, equivariance-based learning objective, and distinct inference computation (convolution, group-convolution, message-passing, gauge-equivariant operators respectively).

This matters for your project in two ways. First, it's an existence proof that non-arbitrary, symmetry-derived taxonomies of computation are achievable — your instinct that categories should come from structure, not discipline, is not a new instinct; it has a five-year-old research program behind it with real traction (it now shows up as prior art in adjacent papers on equivariant networks, information geometry, and category-theoretic treatments of GNNs). Second — and this is the important caveat — Bronstein et al.'s taxonomy is a taxonomy of *data geometry*, not of *knowledge or cognition*. It tells you what symmetry a signal has (an image has translation symmetry, a molecule has permutation symmetry, a mesh has gauge symmetry). It does not address whether reasoning, memory, or compositional inference are separate computational categories. That's the actual gap in your research question, and it's the part that isn't already solved.

**The strongest counter-evidence you have to confront directly**

Before I let you build any taxonomy, you need to sit with the strongest argument against your entire premise: DeepMind's Gato and related generalist-agent work showed a single transformer, with one set of weights and one tokenization scheme, performing competently across image captioning, Atari play, robotic control, and dialogue. If one architecture handles perceptual, sequential-decision, and symbolic tasks passably well, that's direct evidence *against* the claim that different knowledge classes strictly *require* different computation — at minimum it shows one architecture can approximate several classes, even if not optimally. Your research program needs a falsifiable answer to "required" versus "merely more sample-efficient." Right now RP-001 doesn't distinguish these, and it should — a taxonomy where the categories are more efficient but not necessary is a much weaker (though still useful) scientific claim than one where they're provably necessary (as equivariance is, provably, for certain symmetry groups).

**Proposed candidate classes — evaluated against your four criteria**

*Class 1 — Structural/symmetry-derived (Bronstein's classes).* Fundamental: yes, provably — representation theory shows you cannot get equivariance to a symmetry group from a generic architecture without either hard-coding it or paying an exponential sample-complexity cost. Appears across vision, chemistry, 3D geometry, social networks, physics simulation. Not reducible: proven, not intuited. Counterargument you must weigh: recent work shows ensembles can provably learn equivariance through data augmentation alone, without architectural hard-coding — meaning even this most rigorously justified class may be a spectrum (efficiency gradient) rather than a hard binary requirement. Verdict: strongest candidate class, already substantially validated in the literature — but keep it, and cite it, rather than rediscover it.

*Class 2 — Compositional/algorithmic vs. statistical/distributional knowledge.* This maps onto the classical Fodor & Pylyshyn systematicity critique of connectionism (1988): symbolic, rule-governed knowledge (syntax, arithmetic, logic, program structure) exhibits systematic generalization that plain statistical pattern-matching historically failed at (see the SCAN benchmark failures, Lake & Baroni). Appears across language grammar, mathematics, program synthesis, planning — and cross-cuts modality, which is exactly the kind of evidence you want (it isn't "language vs. vision," it's a structural property that shows up inside both). Distinct representation: discrete/structured (trees, programs, logic forms) vs. continuous embeddings. Distinct objective: exact generalization from few examples vs. distributional fit from many. Distinct inference: search/symbolic execution vs. forward pass. Caveat, and I mean this as a real caveat, not a hedge: this is an *actively contested* distinction. Chain-of-thought and in-context learning in large transformers have closed much of the systematicity gap empirically, and it remains unresolved in the literature whether this reflects genuine emergent symbolic computation or increasingly sophisticated statistical interpolation. Do not adopt this class as settled — adopt it as a live hypothesis with a real academic fight attached.

*Class 3 — Memory dynamics: episodic vs. semantic vs. procedural.* This is your best-grounded candidate, because it's the one place cognitive science and ML architecture independently converge on the same triad. Tulving's cognitive taxonomy separates one-shot instance memory (episodic), generalized consolidated knowledge (semantic), and skill/action memory (procedural) — and there is real neuroscience evidence for distinct substrates (hippocampus for episodic encoding, neocortex for semantic consolidation, basal ganglia/cerebellum for procedural learning), not just architectural convenience. The ML analogs are concrete and already built: retrieval-augmented / external-memory architectures for episodic-like lookup, standard parametric weight-training for semantic consolidation, RL policies for procedural/sequential-decision skill. Distinct learning objectives (one-shot write vs. slow gradient consolidation vs. reward-based policy optimization) and distinct inference computations (content-addressable retrieval vs. feedforward mapping vs. rollout/planning) are genuinely different, not merely relabeled. This is the strongest fundamental candidate on your list.

**What I'd explicitly reject**

Anything phrased as "visual knowledge," "linguistic knowledge," or "creative vs. analytical knowledge" — these are modality or discipline labels smuggled back in under new names, exactly what you were warned against. A knowledge graph and a syntax tree are both compositional-class objects despite being different "domains"; an image classification task and an image segmentation task can sit in different classes despite sharing a modality. If a proposed class name maps cleanly onto an academic subject, that's a signal to reject or re-derive it structurally.

**Smallest defensible taxonomy, honestly**

Given the evidence bar you set (cross-domain appearance, distinct representation, distinct objective, distinct inference, non-trivial reducibility), I can currently defend two classes with real theoretical backing (structural/symmetry, memory dynamics) and flag one as a contested third (compositional/statistical) that the literature has not resolved. I would not claim more than that yet — a taxonomy that reaches for four or five categories right now would be intuition dressed as science.

**Falsification experiments before you add any class to the accepted list**

For Class 1: check whether data-augmented generic architectures close the gap with hard-coded equivariant ones as compute scales — if the gap vanishes, this class is an efficiency artifact, not a computational necessity, and should be downgraded accordingly. For Class 2: run systematic generalization benchmarks (SCAN-style) against current frontier in-context learners; if performance is now near-ceiling, the symbolic/statistical divide has been substantially closed and the class weakens. For Class 3: check whether retrieval-augmented + parametric hybrid models actually fail differently than pure end-to-end transformers on tasks requiring genuine one-shot episodic recall versus slow consolidation — if there's no measurable difference in failure mode, the neuroscience analogy hasn't transferred to ML and the class is aspirational rather than demonstrated in artificial systems yet.

Memory-dynamics triad — 0.7 fundamental, well-grounded biologically but 0.4 confidence the ML analogs are yet mature enough to call "distinct computation" rather than "distinct architecture choice." Compositional/statistical divide — 0.3 confidence it survives as a clean binary; treat as an open research question, not a taxonomy entry, until you've run the falsification test above.
