---

## Why This Research Exists

Modern AI has demonstrated remarkable capabilities using largely the same computational paradigm across many different domains.

However, during our earlier research into training a foundation model for web and interface design, a recurring question emerged:

> **Should every form of knowledge be represented, learned, and reasoned about using the same computational process?**

This question gradually expanded beyond design.

Rather than asking how to build a better design model, the research evolved into asking whether different forms of knowledge may naturally require different computational treatment.

This evolution led to the creation of the Adaptive Computational Architecture research program.

The purpose of ACA is **not to begin with an architecture**.

Instead, it seeks to derive any future architecture from first principles.

---

## Current Stage of the Research

The project has intentionally stepped away from architecture design.

The current objective is to investigate the computational foundations that any future architecture must inherit.

The current research sprint is therefore focused on one foundational question:

> **What is knowledge from a computational perspective?**

- representations,
- learning algorithms,
- inference,
- memory,
- scheduling,
- or architecture.

Those topics belong to later stages of the research.

---

## Why Defining Knowledge Matters

Every intelligent system ultimately processes knowledge.

Yet the field commonly assumes an implicit definition of knowledge without first establishing one computationally.

If the definition itself is incomplete, every subsequent layer—including representations, learning objectives, inference procedures, and architectures—may inherit incorrect assumptions.

Therefore this research begins at the lowest possible level.

Rather than asking:

> "How should an AI architecture work?"

we first ask:

> "What exactly is the thing that an intelligent system processes?"

Only after establishing a rigorous computational understanding of knowledge can later research investigate:

- intrinsic properties of knowledge,
- possible computational classes,
- representations,
- learning objectives,
- inference computation,
- and finally architecture.

Architecture is therefore treated as a consequence of understanding knowledge, not as the starting point.
